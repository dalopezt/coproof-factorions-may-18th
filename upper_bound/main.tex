**Theorem.** For every natural number $n > 2{,}540{,}160$, the digit-factorial sum of $n$ is strictly less than $n$. That is,
$$\forall\, n \in \mathbb{N},\quad n > 2{,}540{,}160 \implies \sigma(n) < n,$$
where $\sigma(n)$ denotes the sum of the factorials of the decimal digits of $n$.

*Proof.* Let $n \in \mathbb{N}$ with $n > 2{,}540{,}160$. By \textbf{Digit Factorial Bound}, the digit-factorial sum satisfies
$$\sigma(n) \leq 362880 \cdot (\lfloor \log_{10} n \rfloor + 1).$$
By \textbf{Growth Dominates}, the hypothesis $n > 2{,}540{,}160$ is sufficient to guarantee
$$362880 \cdot (\lfloor \log_{10} n \rfloor + 1) < n.$$
Chaining these two inequalities yields $\sigma(n) < n$, as required. $\blacksquare$
